\section{3d mirror symmetry}
\begin{frame}
I thought that what I was working on $\approx$20 years ago was {\bf almost commutative algebras}: non-commutative algebras that became commutative in a limit.  
\begin{block}{}
  In physics terms, you would think about these in terms of a star-product deforming the functions on the phase space of a classical system.  \medskip

  But something special is happening here: since I'm working in an algebraic setting, I can think of these are the holomorphic functions on a complex manifold.  
\end{block}
Taking the classical limit gives me a {\bf hyperk\"ahler} structure on this manifold.  
\end{frame}
\begin{frame}
So far, so math.  But at some point I learned:
\begin{itemize}
  \item This structure arises whenever we have a 3d quantum field theory with a topological twist (by considering the local operators in the twisted theory).
  \item<2-> All of the examples I was studying occured this way!
  \item<3-> If you have a 3d theory has $\mathcal{N}=4$ supersymmetry, it has two natural classes of topological twists $Q_{\color{red} A}$ and $Q_{\color{blue} B}$, and thus two hyperk\"ahler manifolds associated to it:   the {\bf \color{red} Coulomb}  and {\bf \color{blue} Higgs} branches.
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}
\begin{frame}
There is a particularly natural 3d gauge theory $\mathcal{T}(G,N)$ for a complex representation $N$ of a complex reductive group $G$.
In this case, the two branches are not created equal:
  \begin{itemize}
  \item $\MH$ is classical: the hyperk\"ahler quotient
    $\mathbb{H}^n/\!/\!/\!/G_c$ (Hitchin--Karlhede--Lindstr\"om--Ro\v
    cek, 1987).  Mathematicians digested this decades ago.
  \item $\MC$ receives quantum corrections from monopoles.  For
    $\sim$20 years, physicists computed examples of a space with {\bf
      no mathematical definition}.
  \end{itemize}
  \begin{block}{}
    Braverman--Finkelberg--Nakajima (2016): a rigorous definition, but quite technical and difficult to understand.  I should probably emphasize: this definition was new to physicists!
  \end{block}
  A number of my research projects in recent years have focused on understanding this definition and its consequences more deeply.
  \end{frame}


\begin{frame}
  \begin{block}{ 3d mirror symmetry (Intriligator--Seiberg, 1996)}  pairs of
  theories $\mathcal{T},\mathcal{T}^!$ equivalent in a way that swaps
  $Q_{\color{red} A}\leftrightarrow Q_{\color{blue} B}$.  In
  particular:
  \[\MH(\mathcal{T})\cong \MC(\mathcal{T}^!)\qquad
    \MC(\mathcal{T})\cong \MH(\mathcal{T}^!)\]
    \end{block}
  To a physicist: a duality.  To a mathematician: a seemingly inexplicable pairing spaces with no visible relationship.
\begin{block}{}
  Despite the difficulty of understanding this connection, with collaborators, we had guessed a number of examples of dual pairs, and proved some of the consequences of this duality.
\end{block}
\end{frame}


\section{Knots and the planar limit}

\begin{frame}
    \begin{block}{}
  Chern--Simons theory:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Defined by a ``sum over histories'' (path integral) where the notion of history is a connection on a principal $G$-bundle over a 3-manifold $M$.
    \item Given a knot $K\subset M$ and a representation $V$ of $G$, we can define the Wilson loop observable $W_{K,V}$, which is a expected value of the trace of the holonomy of the connection around $K$ in the representation $V$.
  \end{itemize}  
  \end{block}  

  Witten (1989): expectation values of Wilson loops in Chern--Simons
  theory are polynomials in a parameter $q$; $\SU2$ with the fundamental
  representation gives the Jones polynomial.

  \vspace{1in}
  % picture: a knot (trefoil?) drawn as a Wilson loop in a solid ball,
  % maybe with the CS action floating next to it


\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
  \begin{block}{Question}
    How does the invariant for $\bigwedge^{\!m}\C^N$ over $\SU N$ 
    vary as a function of $m,N,q$? \bigskip

    There is expected to be a recursion in $m$ and $N$ (the {\bf quantum $\hat{A}$-polynomial}).
  \end{block}

  Physics: 
  \begin{itemize}
    \item should be particularly simple in the {\bf planar ('t Hooft) limit}:
  \[N\to\infty,\qquad q\to 1,\qquad g=q^N \text{ fixed}\]
\item should have a holographic dual description as a topological string on the resolved conifold, with a brane on the conormal Lagrangian of the knot.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
This dual story has also been studied mathematically, and has its own invariants: the {\bf augmentation variety} of the knot, defined by counting holomorphic curves with boundary on the brane.  This is a Lagrangian subvariety of $(\C^*)^2$ (or $(\C^*)^3$ if we include $g$).  


\begin{block}{}
  Thus we have two independently defined invariants of a knot: the augmentation variety, and the planar limit of the quantum $\hat{A}$-polynomial.  Aganagi\'c--Vafa (2012) predicted that these are equal.
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
  Idea (after Gaiotto--Kannagi--Sanjurjo): treat the coloring $m$ as a
  {\bf formal variable}.  While this does not make sense as a representation of $\SU N$, we can still make skein theoretic calculations.
  \bigskip

For the unknot, substituting $g=q^N$, $\mu=q^m$ (no $N$, no $m$!):
\[  \tikz[baseline]{\draw[->,very thick] (0,0.5) node[above] {$m$} arc (90:-315:0.5cm);} =\frac{q-q^{-1}}{\mu-\mu} \,  \tikz[baseline]{\draw[->,very thick] (0,0.5)  arc (90:-315:0.5cm); \node at (.75,-.1){$m$}; \node at (-1,.1){$m-1$}; \draw[mid<] (0,0.5) -- (0,-.5);}=\frac{qg\mu^{-1} -q^{-1}g^{-1}\mu}{\mu-\mu^{-1}}\lambda\, \tikz[baseline]{\draw[->,very thick] (0,0.5) node[above] {$m$} arc (90:-315:0.5cm);}\;. \]
  So $\mu-\mu^{-1}-\la\,(qg\mu^{-1}-q^{-1}g^{-1}\mu)$ annihilates the
  unknot: its deformed $\hat{A}$-polynomial, with no computation of
  any knot invariant.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
  \begin{block}{Theorem (W.--Zaimi, 2026)}
    At $q=1$, the HOMFLYPT difference module {\bf is} the degree-0
    abelianized knot contact homology of $K$.
  \end{block}
  \begin{itemize}
  \item The proof is purely algebraic: diagram calculus.  No
    holomorphic curves, no path integrals.  Elementary tools,
    non-elementary conclusion.
  \item First explicit mathematical connection between these two worlds.
  \end{itemize}
  This paper owes a large debt to conversations with Davide
  Gaiotto---the kind of accident that only happens in this building.
\end{frame}

\section{Coda}
\begin{frame}
Just two examples of these sorts of connections:
  \begin{itemize}
  \item Physics predicted equalities between independently defined
    objects: Higgs $\leftrightarrow$ Coulomb; recursions
    $\leftrightarrow$ augmentation varieties.
  \item Proving them required inventing structures that now have lives of
    their own.
  \end{itemize}
\begin{block}{}
  Physics and mathematics have a few centuries of profitable interaction behind them, and yet we keep finding new ways to surprise each other.  
\end{block}
\end{frame}
